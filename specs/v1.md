# Reflo — Version 1 Spec

A reading companion that quizzes you on non-fiction books, chapter by chapter, to help you genuinely understand and retain what you read. Not gamified. Designed to be simple enough that a five-year-old could use it.

*The name is "reflect" + "loop/flow": the app reflects your understanding back to you and runs the loop again, getting sharper from what your own answers reveal.*

---

## The whole loop (this is the entire app)

1. **Add a book.** Upload an EPUB.
2. **Open the book.** You see its chapter list — the same one printed in your physical copy. You glance at it to confirm it matches.
3. **Tap the chapter you just finished.** That's how you tell the app "this much." No page numbers, no pasting, no highlighting. The app grabs the text between that chapter's start and the next.
4. **Quiz me.** The app asks a handful of multiple-choice questions about that chapter.
5. **You tap an answer.** If you miss, the app shows a short paragraph that mends the idea — and a button to *show me another example* if you want one.
6. **Narrate what you learned.** You say a short summary out loud. This is required, not optional — it's how the app actually sees whether the ideas connected in your head.
7. **The app talks back.** In words only — no score. It acknowledges what connected, gently names what didn't (often as a question, not a verdict), and offers another example if it'd help.

That's it. Come back any time and tap an earlier chapter to quiz it again.

---

## Locked decisions

**Format:** EPUB only. Real text, defined reading order, built-in chapter list.

**"How much I read":** Tap a chapter from the book's own table of contents. The book tells the app where each chapter begins and ends.

**Books:** Non-fiction focus. The book's ideas are meant for the real world, so there's no separate "story world" to test inside.

**Question type:** Multiple choice. The wrong answers are *traps* — each one is a specific, tempting way of misunderstanding the chapter, not random noise. The aim is to test whether ideas *connect*, not to test trivia or memory.

**On a miss:** A short paragraph that finishes the half-formed thought, built around the book's own example.

**Examples:** Use the book's example by default. A *generate another example* button creates a second one only when you tap it — and it appears *next to* the book's example, so you can see what the two share. (One example alone transfers poorly; two compared is what builds understanding you can actually use.)

**Narration:** After the quiz, you narrate a short spoken summary. The app reads your *connected picture* from this — taps can only show whether you caught the pieces; only your own words show whether the pieces joined up.

**Feedback style:**
- Words only. No number, no score, no progress bar. Nothing gamified.
- Talks about your *thinking*, never about you ("you're smart" / "great job" actually backfire).
- Nudges as questions where it can, not verdicts.
- A little genuine encouragement first, then the honest correction.

**Quiz any earlier chapter:** You can tap any chapter you've already read and quiz it again. This is free — it's the same mechanism as quizzing the latest one.

---

## Explicitly OUT of v1 (deliberately deferred)

- **Automatic spaced repetition / resurfacing.** The app won't track your past answers, score you over time, or decide on its own to bring an old chapter back. You go back manually when you want to.
- **PDFs and other formats.** EPUB only for now.
- **Pointing you back to the exact spot** a question came from. Dropped as too complex for v1.
- **Active steering** on a detected gap (auto re-asking, auto-pushing examples, pulling up earlier chapters unprompted). V1 only *reflects* the gap in words and *offers* an example — it doesn't grab the wheel.

---

## Three judgment calls I made to fill the gaps — veto any of these

1. **Spoken narration, not typed.** You valued the talking format (it doubles as rehearsal for explaining the idea to a real person). This adds speech-to-text to the build. If that's too much for a first cut, the fallback is to let v1 accept typed narration and add voice later.
2. **Light-touch response to a gap.** When the app sees your picture is incomplete, v1 just *says so in words* and *offers* an example — it does not steer, resurface, or re-quiz. The simplest coherent version of "the app runs the check."
3. **Quiz earlier chapters is IN, automatic resurfacing is OUT.** The cheap, manual version of retention ships in v1 because it costs almost nothing; the expensive, automatic version waits.

---

## The honest hard parts (so you build with eyes open)

These aren't blockers — they're where the quality will live or die, worth
knowing before you start:

- **Writing genuinely tempting MCQ traps**, every chapter, every time. Lazy distractors turn a deep question back into trivia. This is the heart of the app.
- **Responding well to the narration** — warm-then-honest, question-shaped, no judgment of the person. This is the single hardest thing the LLM has to do, and your design makes it load-bearing.
- **Voice input**, if you keep spoken narration.
- **Messy EPUBs.** Most are clean; some have weird chapter lists. When a book is built oddly, the "tap a chapter" experience gets odd too. Acceptable for v1.
